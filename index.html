Stage 01 of 11

Ticket submitted

A request enters the process from the client or internal team — via direct email, an email forward to Monday, or in-meeting. This is the trigger for everything that follows.

📌
How requests arrive
Three common entry points: (1) client emails Steve or the team directly, (2) client or internal team submits to Monday via email forward, (3) a verbal request surfaces on a sync call and needs to be captured manually. The first two trigger Monday automations. The third requires manual ticket creation.

What's happening: Caiden is tagging both Steve and Adam directly in a reply chain. No formal ticket yet — this is the raw moment of request entry.

Stage 03 of 11

Auto-response sent

Monday's automation fires immediately when a ticket is created — sending a confirmation update back to the item and notifying the assigned owner (Steve) that a new request has been logged.

🤖
What the automation does
Monday fires two automatic actions: (1) posts a formatted update to the item thread thanking the requester and logging the submission details, (2) assigns Steve as owner and sends an assignment notification email. This creates an audit trail before any human action is taken.

Stage 04 of 11

PM confirm-receipt

Steve sends a human confirmation back to the client — acknowledging the request, linking the Monday item, and setting expectations. This is the first human touchpoint after automation.

✉️
The confirm-receipt pattern
Every confirmation email follows the same structure: (1) thank the requester by name, (2) confirm receipt explicitly, (3) share the Monday task link for reference, (4) set a clear expectation on next steps. The Monday hyperlink is critical — it closes the loop between email and board.

Pattern in action: Named greeting → confirm receipt → clear next step ("provide an update shortly") → leaves door open. Response time from Caiden's email: ~8 minutes.

Stage 05 of 11

Triage: simple vs. complex

Based on priority and complexity, the request is assessed internally. Simple requests go straight into the weekly sprint. Complex requests get scoped (S / M / L) and scheduled accordingly.

Complex path
Scoped: S / M / L
Requires full dev review. A rough scope gauge is assigned before dev begins. Work is scheduled based on capacity and backlog.
Example: Milgard OIC → Jitterbit migration, Western Windows new site, BazaarVoice feed rebuild
Simple path
Weekly sprint queue
Adam picks these up in the regular sprint cadence. Fast turnaround, minimal coordination overhead.
Example: PGT tech resources error, C650 product removal, copy overrides
🔀
How triage happens in practice
There's no formal scoring system — triage happens through conversation between Steve and Adam. The Monday board due date, priority column, and any scope notes added to the item's update thread serve as the running record. Critical/urgent items get flagged immediately in a separate email or Slack to Adam outside the formal board flow.
Real example — urgent escalation to Adam (C650)

The real triage moment: This internal forward to Adam is where true urgency gets communicated. Steve flags the 6pm deadline explicitly — this is outside the formal Monday flow. The informal escalation channel (direct email to Adam) is a key part of how urgent items actually get prioritized.

Stage 06 of 11

Dev builds + notifies BOL

Adam's team implements the work and sends an internal update — either via Monday, email, or both — letting BOL know the build is ready for review before it goes to the client.

🛠️
Dev communication pattern
Adam typically sends a brief, direct email with: what was done, a staging link or environment credentials if applicable, and any notes or caveats. If there are blockers or questions, he flags them here before BOL review — not after the client sees it.

Stage 07 of 11

BOL internal review

Before the work reaches the client, BOL reviews it internally. This is the quality gate — Steve (and where relevant, Sarah or Meredith) checks the work and either approves it to move forward or routes feedback back to Adam.

🔍
What BOL checks for
Functional accuracy (does it do what was asked?), brand/content alignment (does it match client expectations?), and scope check (did anything unexpected surface?). If there's a blocker or mismatch, BOL escalates internally before the client ever sees it. This stage protects the client relationship.

Internal flag before client: Adam surfaced a data mismatch (redirect sheets don't match live URLs) directly to the BOL team first — not to the client. This is the internal review gate working correctly: catching issues before they reach Nick's team.

Stage 08 of 11

PM sends to client

Once BOL approves the work, Steve sends it to the client for review — via email, a Monday update, or both. The dual-channel approach ensures nothing falls through the cracks.

📤
Dual-channel delivery
For most deliverables, Steve sends a Monday update (which emails the watchers) AND a direct email — especially for anything with a staging link, credentials, or feedback deadline. The Monday thread creates the audit trail; the direct email ensures visibility for client stakeholders who may not watch Monday closely.
Real example — Sunrise Triton landing page sent for client review
SS
Steve → Nick (via Monday update)
[New update] "Sunrise | Web Dev | Triton..." — Hi Nick, hope you had a nice weekend...
BOL May 11
Hi @Nick Board - hope you had a nice weekend 👋 [Monday update sharing the Triton landing page build for review] Please review and let us know any feedback — Hallie and Caiden are also tagged for visibility.
Monday-first delivery: Steve posted the client-facing update directly on the Monday item, which triggers email notifications to all watchers (Nick, Hallie, Caiden). This is the Monday-as-comms-channel pattern in action.

Stage 09 of 11

Client review

The client reviews the delivered work. Two paths: they approve and we move to deployment, or they provide feedback and we route back to Adam's team to rectify.

Approved
→ Deployment queue
Client signs off. Work is queued for production deployment — typically that night or the following morning, or batched if there are multiple items.
Feedback received
→ Back to dev
Client feedback is consolidated and routed back to Adam's team. The loop repeats from Stage 6 until approval is reached.

Stage 10 of 11

Deployed to production

Once client approval is received, the work is deployed to the live site — typically overnight or the morning after approval, or batched if there are multiple items queued that week.

🚀
Deployment cadence
Standard deployment is night-of or morning-after client approval. When there's a queue of items, Curt or Adam batches them into a single deployment window. Adam or Curt sends a confirmation note — either via Monday update or direct email — once the code is live. Steve then sends the final confirmation to the client.

[From meeting recap, May 11 Dealer Portal sync] Curt moved PGT lightbox content to production and intends to push the code live tonight. Action item: Curt Loughin will push the PGT lightbox/code live tonight.
Batched deployment confirmed in sync: Content is already in production; the code push is the final step. The meeting recap creates the deployment record. Steve will follow up with client confirmation once it's live.

Stage 11 of 11

Task closed

Steve confirms deployment to the client, the Monday item is marked complete, and the task is officially closed — unless the client comes back with post-deployment feedback, which restarts the dev loop.

✅
Closure pattern
Closure requires two things: (1) Steve sends the client a confirmation that the work is live, and (2) the Monday item status is updated to Done/Complete. If the client comes back with post-deployment issues, the item re-opens and routes back to dev before final close. The goal is a clean paper trail from submission to closure.

Client-initiated closure signal: Mary commits to verifying and then greenlit for production. This is the hand-off moment that moves the item into the deployment queue. Steve stays CC'd to track.

Hiccups & friction points

Where the process breaks down, slows down, or goes off-script in practice. Pulled from real email threads and meeting recaps. These are the talking points for the improvement conversation.

01
Verbal requests that never get ticketed
Submission gap · Drupal + Portal boards
Process gap
▸

A meaningful portion of requests surface verbally on sync calls — the CRO "More to explore" module, the BazaarVoice feed issues, Western Windows dealer transition scope — and don't land in a Monday item until after the call, if at all. When that step gets skipped, there's no audit trail, no assignment, and no auto-routing.

From the May 11 BOL/BT dev sync recap
"Meredith flagged the 'More to explore' module as a higher-priority CRO dev item. Adam requested links to pages showing the inconsistent module behavior to assist troubleshooting."
Impact: Items discussed in meetings may not get ticketed promptly, creating a gap between what the team agreed to and what's tracked in Monday.
💬 Discussion prompt: Should we require a Monday item to be created before a call ends for any new work surfaced verbally?
02
Informal escalation channel to Adam outside Monday
Communication gap · Urgent items
Comms gap
▸

When something is truly urgent, Steve forwards directly to Adam by email — outside the Monday board flow. This works, but it means the urgency signal lives in an inbox thread rather than on the board. Anyone looking at Monday won't see that this item was flagged as a same-day emergency.

Real example — C650 urgent escalation (Apr 28)
Steve → Adam, Sarah: "Hey Adam -- sorry but it seems Jen is forwarding another urgent item that needs to be done today by 6pm ET..."
Impact: Urgency context is siloed in email. If Adam is unavailable and someone else checks Monday, they have no idea this was a 6pm-today deadline.
💬 Discussion prompt: Should urgent escalations include a Monday priority flag update in addition to the direct email? Or is a dedicated "HOT" label/group worth adding to the board?
03
Client scope reversals mid-ticket
Scope management · Western Windows
Scope creep
▸

Tickets occasionally reverse scope after they've been confirmed and queued. The C650 thread is a clean example — Jen submitted a removal request, Steve confirmed and queued it, then Jen came back with a reversal and a new, different ask. The Monday item reflects the original request, not the updated one.

Real example — C650 scope reversal (Apr 28)
Jen → Steve (follow-up, ~48 min later): "Hi Steve, Thanks for your email. There has been a reversal of the requested tasks. They do not want technical resources removed, so those are fine. However, here are the issues that I am having: [new scope]..." Steve → Jen: "Thank you Jen -- confirming receipt. I've added this to monday for further internal review + tracking."
Impact: The original Monday item may not reflect the current ask. Dev could build the wrong thing if they pull from the original ticket without reading the full update thread.
💬 Discussion prompt: Should scope reversals require closing the original item and opening a new one? Or is an update comment with a clear "REVISED SCOPE" flag sufficient?
04
Dependency stalls waiting on client decisions
Blocker management · Portal + Drupal
Blocker
▸

Several active items are stalled not because of dev capacity, but because a client-side decision hasn't been made. The OIC-to-Jitterbit conversion has no response from the client. The order-status integration staging is ready but waiting on Joni or Talena to decide whether to proceed. These items sit open on the board with no clear next action.

From the May 11 Dealer Portal sync recap
"The order-status integration is functioning on staging but an upstream field issue requires Joni or Talena to decide whether to proceed or wait for a fix." "Steve will ping Mary for a status update on the OIC-to-Jitterbit conversion because no response has been received yet."
Impact: Board items look "in progress" but are actually blocked. Without a visible blocker status, it's hard to distinguish items that are actively being worked vs. sitting idle waiting on someone else.
💬 Discussion prompt: Should we formalize a "Waiting on Client" status in Monday? And set a follow-up SLA — e.g. if no response in 3 business days, Steve auto-follows up?
05
Scope clarification needed before routing
PM overhead · Dealer Portal
Process gap
▸

Some requests arrive ambiguous enough that Steve needs to clarify scope before he can even route or queue them. The Customer Portal Figma request is a good example — Nick's ask was unclear enough that Steve had to check internally with Sarah first, then go back to Nick for clarification before anything could be logged and assigned.

Real example — Customer Portal Figma (Apr 29–30)
Nick → Steve + Sarah: "Hi Team - Can your creative team build a Figma project of the current state of the portal so we can quickly have our team provide design comps on changes?" Steve → Sarah (internal, before responding to Nick): "Sorry now reading this back - I'm confused and don't think it relates to that Fraud Request for Curt on the Portal… Let me know if you want me to respond to clarify?" Steve → Nick (clarification): "Did you want BOL Creative to put together a Figma File Project of the current Dealer Portal designs? Additionally -- since the UX doesn't change..."
Impact: Adds an extra round-trip before the item can even be queued. Delays the confirm-receipt, delays dev assignment, and creates internal overhead that isn't visible to the client.
💬 Discussion prompt: Would a lightweight intake form (even just a Gravity Form or a Monday intake template) reduce ambiguous submissions from the client side?
